Performance vs Efficiency: A Comparison of Fine-Tuning Strategies for Hate Speech Detection on Social Media



Primary RQ
How do different fine-tuning strategies compare in terms of classification performance and computational cost for hate speech detection on social media text?
Sub-RQs
RQ1 — Performance comparison across paradigms
How large is the performance gap between non-pretrained approaches (classical methods, neural networks trained from scratch) and pretrained approaches (DistilBERT with different adaptation strategies)? Within the pretrained group, do different fine-tuning strategies (frozen backbone, partial fine-tuning, LoRA, LP-FT, full fine-tuning, efficient head fine-tuning) lead to meaningfully different classification performance?
RQ2 — Efficiency-performance trade-off
When accounting for trainable parameter count, training time, and memory usage, do parameter-efficient methods (linear probing, partial fine-tuning, LoRA) offer Pareto-optimal trade-offs against full fine-tuning? In other words, is the additional computational cost of full fine-tuning justified by its performance gains, or can lightweight methods achieve comparable performance at a fraction of the cost?
RQ3 — Two-stage vs single-stage adaptation
Do two-stage methods (LP-FT, efficient head fine-tuning) outperform their single-stage counterparts (full fine-tuning, LoRA) by a meaningful margin, and does the additional training complexity translate into practically useful gains?
Bonus RQ (if you do the general vs domain-specific bonus)
Does a domain-adapted pretrained model (e.g., trained on social media or hateful content) respond differently to the same fine-tuning strategies compared to a general-domain pretrained model like DistilBERT? Does domain-matched pretraining reduce the performance gap between lightweight methods and full fine-tuning?
Optional data-scaling sub-question (if you keep data fractions as bonus)
Do the relative rankings and efficiency trade-offs between fine-tuning strategies remain stable as training data availability varies (5%, 20%, 50%, 100%), or does the optimal strategy shift depending on data availability?

Abstract
The rise of hateful and offensive content on social media has prompted significant research into automatic detection systems. This project systematically compares different fine-tuning methods for hate speech detection on social media text: frozen backbone, partial fine-tuning, full fine-tuning, linear-probing fine-tuning (LP-FT), LoRA, and efficient head fine-tuning. Additionally, each method fine-tunes the pretrained model DistilBERT on a hate speech classification dataset, HateXplain.

TF-IDF embedding with logistic regression, a Bi-LSTM trained from scratch, and a randomly initialised DistilBERT model are included as baselines to contextualise the contribution of fine-tuning.

All methods are evaluated on the HateXplain dataset under identical training and evaluation conditions. We evaluate the classification performance models using F1-score, precision, and recall metrics, and report training time and memory usage to examine the computational cost of each method.

Rather than pursuing state-of-the-art accuracy, we focus on the trade-offs between performance and efficiency, identifying when lightweight methods remain competitive.
